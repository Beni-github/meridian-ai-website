When someone in Bend, Oregon needs a plumber, they don't flip through the Yellow Pages. They open Google and type "plumber near me" or "plumber Bend Oregon." If your plumbing business doesn't show up in those results, you're invisible to a customer who was ready to book.

This is what local SEO solves.

## What Is Local SEO?

Local SEO is the practice of optimizing your business to appear in search results when people search for products or services in their area. Unlike general SEO, which tries to rank websites nationally or globally, local SEO focuses on capturing searchers in your specific city or region.

Think of it as making sure your business shows up when it should. When someone searches "coffee shop Eugene," you want to be there. When someone searches "HVAC repair Vancouver WA," your business should appear.

## Why Your Google Ranking Matters More Than You Think

Most people click on one of the top three results. If you're not in those spots, you're relying on customers to scroll past the businesses that did the work. In competitive markets, this costs you real revenue.

Here's the reality: **72% of consumers who search for something local visit a store within five miles.** Your potential customers are searching right now. The question is whether they find you.

## Your Google Business Profile: Your Digital Storefront

The most important tool for local SEO is your Google Business Profile (formerly Google My Business). It's the panel that appears on the right side of search results when someone searches for your business name — or when Google thinks they're looking for a business like yours.

It shows your address, hours, phone number, reviews, and photos. But more importantly, it signals to Google that you exist, you're legitimate, and you're relevant to local searchers.

**Optimizing it means:**

- Choosing the right business category (and secondary categories)
- Writing a complete, keyword-rich business description
- Adding photos regularly
- Keeping your hours accurate, especially during holidays
- Responding to every review, positive and negative

## Local Keywords: Speaking Your Customer's Language

Your customers aren't searching for "full-stack HVAC solutions." They're searching for "heating repair," "furnace not working," or "AC unit making noise."

Using those natural phrases on your website — in your page titles, headings, and content — tells Google you're relevant to what people are actually looking for. If you're a dentist in Tacoma, phrases like "Tacoma family dentist" or "emergency dentist near Tacoma" matter more than generic industry terms.

## Reviews: The Modern Word of Mouth

Online reviews are social proof. They influence whether someone clicks on your listing and whether they trust you enough to call. Businesses with more positive reviews consistently rank higher in local results.

Encourage your happy customers to leave reviews. Respond to them. A business that engages with its reviews signals to Google — and to potential customers — that it's active and cares about its reputation.

## Citations and NAP Consistency

A citation is any mention of your business name, address, and phone number online — on directories like Yelp, YellowPages, or local chamber of commerce sites.

**NAP consistency** means that information is exactly the same everywhere it appears. If your listing says "123 Main St" on Google but "123 Main Street" on Yelp, Google gets confused. That confusion can hurt your ranking.

Audit your citations. Make sure your business name, address, and phone number match exactly across every platform.

## The Pacific Northwest Advantage

Here's something many business owners in our region don't realize: **competition for local search visibility is lower here than in major metro areas.** Markets like Portland, Seattle, and Boise are growing fast, but smaller Pacific Northwest towns — Eugene, Salem, Spokane, Missoula, Bend — still have relatively low local SEO saturation.

This means the businesses that invest in local SEO now have a real window of opportunity. The businesses that wait may find themselves competing in a more crowded landscape a few years from now.
