If you're a small business owner in the Pacific Northwest — a dentist in Vancouver, a contractor in Portland, a salon owner in Tacoma — someone has probably told you that a Facebook or Instagram page is "enough" for your business online. Maybe you've believed it.

Here's the truth: a social media page is a tool. A website is your foundation.

Social media has its place. But relying on a Facebook page as your only online presence is like running your bookkeeping on a napkin. It might work in a pinch, but it's not built to last — and it doesn't give you control over much.

**Credibility Gaps**

When someone searches for your business — which happens more often than you think, even for word-of-mouth referrals — what do they find? If the answer is just a social profile, you might be losing credibility before you even get a chance to make an impression.

A professional website tells people you're real, you're established, and you care about how you present yourself. Social pages can feel thin, especially when someone is trying to decide whether to trust you with their money or their business.

**The Google Problem**

Here's the part most people miss: social media profiles are hard to find on Google. They don't rank well. They don't show up in地图 results. They don't give you control over what someone sees when your business name is searched.

When a potential customer searches "dentist Beaverton" or "best breakfast Tacoma," you want a website that shows up — not a Facebook page buried on page three. A properly built website with local SEO signals (your business name, address, phone number, service areas) puts you in the game.

**You Don't Control the Platform**

Facebook changes its algorithm constantly. One update can tank your page's visibility overnight. Instagram does the same. You've seen it happen to other businesses — their posts reach fewer people, engagement drops, and suddenly they're scrambling.

Your website doesn't answer to an algorithm. What you build is what people see. No boosted posts required. No pay-to-play pressure.

**What a Good Small Business Website Actually Needs**

A good website doesn't mean an expensive one. But it does mean a website that's:

- **Mobile responsive** — Most local searches happen on phones. If your site looks broken on a phone screen, people leave.
- **Fast** — Every second of load time costs you visitors. Slow sites get abandoned.
- **Easy to contact you** — A phone number, a contact form, or a booking link. No obstacles between you and a new customer inquiry.
- **Set up for local search** — Google Business Profile integration, consistent NAP (name, address, phone) information, and location-specific content.

If you don't know whether your current site hits these marks, that's a fair question to ask someone who does.
